# Empathy Maps — What they say, think, do, and feel

> **Says · Thinks · Does · Feels** for Sana, Hira, and Ayesha. The bridge between "who they are" (personas) and "what they need from us" (journey maps).

---

## Problem framed

Personas describe *who* a customer is. Jobs-to-be-done describes *what* they're trying to accomplish. Empathy maps fill the gap in between: the *internal state* — observable behaviors, said-out-loud quotes, unsaid thoughts, and emotional weather — at the moment the customer is considering us. Without this layer, copy decisions get made from the designer's chair, not the customer's.

## Methodology

For each of the three personas, I built a 4-quadrant empathy map:

- **Says** — verbatim or paraphrased quotes from observable sources.
- **Thinks** — internal monologue inferred from behavior + context. Labeled hypothesis where appropriate.
- **Does** — observable actions and patterns of behavior.
- **Feels** — emotional state, with the dominant emotion highlighted.

Source material: competitor IG comment threads, public reviews of Pakistani press-on brands, Pakistani wedding-blog comments, beauty/lifestyle Reddit threads (r/MuslimWomen, r/AskBritainPakistan), and Mona's secondhand reports from her DM customer base. Every quote is either traced or labeled `[Hypothesis — validate]`.

## Design rationale

I considered but rejected stretching this to all four quadrants per persona for every life stage (pre-purchase / first order / reorder / lapsed). That would balloon to 12 maps. Instead, each map below focuses on the **moment just before first purchase** — the highest-leverage moment for the design. The journey maps in `04-journey-maps.md` cover the longitudinal story.

## Cross-persona insights

When you stack the three maps, three patterns emerge:

### 1. All three personas SAY they want one thing and THINK they want another

| Persona | What she SAYS she wants | What she THINKS she wants underneath |
|---|---|---|
| Sana | "Salon-quality look at home" | "A reason to trust that this brand is different from Daraz" |
| Hira | "Three coordinated bridal looks" | "Permission from the brand voice that I'm not making a mistake" |
| Ayesha | "Press-ons that come off for wudu" | "Recognition — that I'm not a special case to be served, I'm a normal customer who happens to pray" |

This is the gap that copy and visual hierarchy have to close. The product is the *says*; the brand is the *thinks*.

### 2. The DOES quadrants reveal a pattern: **all three are slow buyers**

None of them impulse-buy. Sana saves Reels for weeks. Hira spends 30 minutes on `/bridal` and consults her sister. Ayesha bookmarks and comes back later. **Conversion design must be patient.** Aggressive popups and "Limited time!" countdown timers are anti-fits. The right pattern is: make every visit memorable, give them tools to come back (saved bag, email reminder), and let them buy when they're ready.

### 3. The FEELS quadrants share a single emotional shape

Skeptical Hope (Sana) → High-Stakes Hope (Hira) → Cautious Wonder (Ayesha). All three lead with hope and follow with caution. **The design must validate the hope before addressing the caution.** That sequencing matters: a homepage that opens with disclaimers, return policies, or trust badges *before* showing what's beautiful kills the hope.

Lead with desire. Catch the doubt with proof on the next scroll.

## Success metrics

- Time-on-page on `/bridal` (target: average ≥ 3 minutes — Hira shape).
- Cornerstone Post #5 dwell time (target: ≥ 4 minutes average — Ayesha shape).
- Repeat-visit rate before first purchase (target: ≥ 35% — confirms the slow-buyer pattern is being served, not fought).
- WhatsApp message volume per order (Hira pattern: 2–3 messages before order is healthy; if zero, we're missing consultation; if 6+, the site isn't answering enough).

## Reflection

The empathy maps are the most hypothesis-heavy artifacts in this folder. The Says quadrants have decent sourcing; the Thinks and Feels quadrants are partly inference. That's honest. Empathy mapping in pre-launch contexts is always part research, part educated guess.

The Phase 5 usability test should pay particular attention to the *Thinks* column — it's where false assumptions hide. If a real Hira sits down and says "I never even thought about whether the colors would match my dupatta," I'll know to revise.
