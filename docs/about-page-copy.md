## Section 1: Where it all began

From the time I was a little girl in Mirpur, I was always the one with a pencil in her hand. Drawing on the margins of notebooks. Decorating whatever I could find. Art wasn't something I chose — it was just the way I saw the world.

Through school, I took part in every design activity I could. It was never about winning. It was about making something with my hands that hadn't existed before.

---

## Section 2: University — where it became real

When the time came to choose a degree, Bachelor of Arts was the only answer. It was the most natural thing in the world for someone like me.

At university, I didn't just study — I worked. I outperformed my colleagues in project after project. Not because I was competitive, but because I genuinely loved every task. From bridal mehndi to acrylic designs, from paintings to handcrafted resin name plates and proxy items — I tried everything. And I was good at most of it.

That time taught me one important thing: I don't have just one art. I have many. And they all live together in the same pair of hands.

---

## Section 3: The problem I couldn't ignore

As a practicing Muslim, I've always wanted beautiful nails. But traditional nail polish and acrylic extensions aren't compatible with wudu — the Islamic ritual of washing before prayer. Water has to reach the nail bed, and a solid coat of polish or acrylic doesn't allow that.

For years I watched other women in the same position. Wanting to feel put-together. Wanting to celebrate a wedding or an Eid morning with nails they loved. But choosing not to — or feeling guilty when they did.

I thought: there has to be a better way.

Press-on nails can be removed before wudu and reapplied after. That's it. That's the whole solution. And nobody was talking about it — at least not in Pakistan, and not in a way that felt honest and made for us.

---

## Section 4: Two years of building something real

About two years ago, I almost didn't start. I had the skills, I had the ideas — but not the belief in myself. That changed when my family encouraged me to try. Not just once, but consistently. They saw something in me that I was still learning to see in myself.

So I started small. A personal hobby. A few sets for friends. A first Instagram post that felt terrifying to publish.

Order by order, the trust grew. Customers from Lahore, Karachi, Islamabad, and places in between. Women who came back for their second set, their third, their sister's bridal order. Every reorder felt like a vote of confidence.

---

## Section 5: What I promise you

I still make every set myself. No factory. No shortcuts. When you order from Nails by Mona, the hands that touch your nails are mine.

I measure properly, so your nails actually fit. I use materials that last. And I care about what you think — because your trust is the only reason any of this works.
